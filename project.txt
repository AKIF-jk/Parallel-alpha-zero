1. Project Overview
The semester project is designed to give students hands-on experience with a real research
problem in the area of Parallel and Distributed Computing (PDC). Each group will select
one research paper from an approved list and use that paper as the base reference for a
scoped implementation and performance-oriented study.
The project focuses on the concepts studied in the course specially parallelization strat-
egy, and computational performance. Students are expected to:
1. understand the computational problem and its relevance,
2. study the core idea proposed in the paper,
3. identify an implementable scope and a potential gap or opportunity for improvement,
4. explore suitable optimizations or parallelization strategies within the level of this
course, and propose the solution (Methodology)
5. implement the solution and perform necessary experiments to test your hypothesis.
6. evaluate behavior through experiments and technical analysis.
7. write report in the form of paper draft.
The project should demonstrate clear understanding of the relationship between al-
gorithm design and system performance, specifically regarding decomposition, work-
load characteristics, memory behavior, parallel execution, and bottlenecks that influence
speedup and efficiency.
2. Project Theme and Expected Directions
The project must remain centered on computational performance, scalability, and
parallelization. Suitable directions include, but are not limited to:
1• SIMD intrinsics and vectorization
• Parallel algorithm design (shared memory)
• Distributed algorithm design (message passing)
• GPU acceleration (OpenCL / OpenCL)
• Hybrid solutions (e.g., MPI + OpenMP + SIMD)
• Memory optimization and cache-aware computation
• Task scheduling and load balancing
• Data locality and memory hierarchy optimization
• Fault tolerance in distributed systems
• Performance benchmarking and bottleneck analysis
• Scalability and efficiency studies
The selected paper must clearly relate to at least one of the above directions.
3. Group Formation
The project will be completed in groups of two students only of the same section. Both
members are expected to contribute equally to reading, implementation, experimentation,
and presentation. During evaluations, both students must be able to explain the selected
paper, the implementation scope, and the performance results.
4. Base Paper Selection
A Google Sheet containing the list of approved papers is shared on GCR. Each group
must select one paper from this list as its base paper.
The provided papers have been carefully curated to ensure relevance to the course, ap-
propriate technical depth, and diversity across different areas of Parallel and Distributed
Computing. However, the responsibility of selecting a suitable paper lies with the group.
The chosen paper must satisfy the following criteria:
• it must be clearly aligned with the core themes of the course (parallelism, performance,
scalability, or optimization),
2• it must be technically meaningful and sufficiently understood by both group members,
• it must be feasible to study, scope, and implement within the given project timeline.
The selected paper serves as the conceptual foundation. Students are encouraged to un-
derstand the original problem, identify the central contribution, recognize computational
challenges, and define a realistic project direction inspired by that work.

36. Milestone 1: Project Selection (Week 1, Due Day 3)
Each group must submit its selected paper using the Google Form shared on Google
Classroom within the first three days.
The form submission must include:
1. Names and roll numbers of both group members
2. Selected base paper title (from the approved list)
3. A brief description (150–250 words) covering:
• What problem does the paper address?
• What is the core computational or parallelization idea?
• Why did you choose this paper?
Note: A vague or generic summary will indicate weak understanding and may result in
the group being asked to revise or change their selection.
7. Milestone 2: Paper Understanding Presentation (Week 2)
Each group will present (or submit recording) its selected paper in Week 2. This mile-
stone verifies that the group has genuinely understood the paper and has a technically
sound plan for the project.
The presentation should cover:
1. Introduction: What is the problem area and why is it important?
2. Core Idea: What is the central contribution of the paper, understanding (according
to the problem):
• What kind of parallelism exists in the problem?
• What parallelization strategy (at different stages studied in the class, from de-
composition to orchestration) is opted?
• How Data Locality/Cache Utilization is handled?
3. Approach: How does the paper solve the problem? What parallelization / optimiza-
tion strategy is used?
44. Gap / Opportunity: What limitation, simplification, or further opportunity do you
identify?
5. Proposed Aproach: What part will you implement, adapt, optimize, or evaluate?
6. Preliminary Plan: Tools, languages, platforms, and rough implementation plan.
Groups should be prepared to answer technical questions (as discussed in the course)
such as:
• What kind of parallelism exists in this problem?
• Where is the expected bottleneck?
• Is the workload compute-bound, memory-bound, or communication-bound?
• What part of the paper is realistic to implement at this course level?

9. Expected Technical Focus
Since this is a PDC project, technical evaluation will particularly value evidence of
thoughtful work in:
• Problem decomposition (data vs. task parallelism)
• Work distribution and load balancing
• Synchronization and communication considerations
• Vectorization / SIMD opportunities
• Memory layout, locality, and cache-aware design
• Scheduling choices and their impact
• Bottleneck identification (compute, memory, communication)
• Speedup and efficiency analysis
• Robustness and reproducibility of results
Students are expected to go beyond simply reporting execution times. Strong projects
will explain why performance improves or fails to improve.
